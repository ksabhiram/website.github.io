Must Read February 2025
On "The Character of Physical Law" — Feynman's Most Underrated Book
Not the Lectures, not QED. This slim volume — transcribed from BBC talks — captures something the textbooks miss: the texture of how physicists actually think.
Open Full Article ↗

Feynman delivered these lectures at Cornell in 1964, and they were broadcast by the BBC. The transcript was published as a slim book — easy to overlook next to the three-volume Lectures on Physics. That would be a mistake.

What makes The Character of Physical Law special is not its technical content (there is almost none) but its epistemic content. Feynman explains what it feels like to do physics: the provisional nature of physical laws, the interplay between mathematics and physical intuition, the strange effectiveness of symmetry arguments. These are things rarely taught explicitly, even in graduate programmes.

Rereading it mid-PhD, I was struck by how much of what Feynman describes I had absorbed implicitly without ever having it named. Chapter 6, on the relation between mathematics and physics, is worth reading every year. The discussion of what it means to "explain" something — as opposed to merely describing it — should be required reading for anyone writing a thesis.

Paper December 2024
Klein et al. (2023): Rethinking the Kubelka-Munk Validity Condition
Why this methodological paper should be required reading for anyone running diffuse reflectance measurements on narrow-gap semiconductors.
Open Full Article ↗

Klein et al. derive an explicit validity criterion for the Kubelka-Munk transformation: the ratio of the absorption coefficient to the scattering coefficient must be much less than one. For narrow-gap semiconductors — PbTe, SnSe, Bi₂Te₃ — this condition is frequently violated near the band edge, precisely where you are trying to extract the gap.

The practical upshot is that K-M Tauc plots in these materials can shift the apparent band edge by tens to hundreds of meV. The paper provides a diagnostic procedure: comparing the K-M Tauc plot with the direct (Tauc plot of (1−R)²) reveals whether the transformation is safe. If they diverge in slope or intercept, K-M is failing.

I have used this criterion extensively in my own work on PbTe-based alloys. The Klein diagnostic is now part of my standard UV-Vis analysis pipeline, and I report both Tauc plots when there is any ambiguity.

Book October 2024
Ashcroft & Mermin vs. Kittel: Two Views of Solid State Physics
A comparison for graduate students: which book to read first, which to annotate, and which to reach for when you're genuinely stuck.
Open Full Article ↗

Kittel is a survey. Ashcroft & Mermin is an argument. That distinction matters more than it might seem when you are trying to develop physical intuition rather than just pass an exam.

Kittel introduces topics efficiently but rarely lingers on the why. A&M takes the opposite approach: it derives rather than states, and it is explicit about what assumptions are being made and when they might fail. For someone working on transport phenomena — thermoelectrics, Hall effect, magnetoresistance — A&M's treatment of the Boltzmann equation and relaxation time approximation is simply irreplaceable.

My recommendation: read Kittel first to get the landscape, then work through A&M chapter by chapter as you encounter topics in your research. Keep Kittel as a quick-reference dictionary; use A&M when you need to actually understand something.

Must Read August 2024
Snyder & Toberer (2008) at 16: A Retrospective
The most-cited thermoelectrics review. What it got right, what the field has corrected, and what the figure-of-merit debate still gets wrong.
Open Full Article ↗

Snyder & Toberer's 2008 Nature Materials review introduced the concept of the band-convergence and nanostructuring strategies to a broad audience. It is the first citation in almost every thermoelectrics paper written since. Rereading it 16 years on is instructive in several ways.

What it got right: the framework of decoupling electronic and thermal transport, the emphasis on the quality factor β, and the argument that zT > 2 was achievable without violating any fundamental constraint. What has been revised: the role of lone-pair electrons in PbTe band convergence is now better understood; the Seebeck coefficient models have been substantially refined; and the community has largely moved away from "phonon-glass electron-crystal" as an organising principle.

Book June 2024
"How to Think Like a Mathematician" — A Researcher's Retrospective
Kevin Houston's book is aimed at undergraduates. Rereading it mid-PhD, I found it surprisingly diagnostic of the habits I'd not yet formed.
Open Full Article ↗

Houston's book is about mathematical proof and rigour, pitched at undergraduates encountering formal mathematics for the first time. Its relevance to experimental physics is not immediately obvious. But its central theme — learning to distinguish what you know from what you assume — is as important in the lab as in the lecture hall.

The chapter on definitions is quietly devastating: Houston points out that most errors in mathematics come not from faulty logic but from using terms without having first made precise what they mean. I recognise this failure constantly in my own writing. "Optimal carrier concentration", "dominant scattering mechanism", "band gap" — these phrases get used as if their meaning were fixed, when often it is context-dependent and under-specified.

Before we dive in, I want to say a word about why this question matters. Eugene Wigner wrote a famous essay in 1960 called "The Unreasonable Effectiveness of Mathematics in the Natural Sciences." He was genuinely puzzled. Why should abstract structures invented by mathematicians — often with no physical application in mind — turn out to describe nature so precisely?
